# The Decision Framework That Was Built for Career Crossroads

Monday I made the case that the Fire Horse year disrupts careers — specifically the ones where autopilot has been running across dimensions people haven't been watching. If that landed for you, this post is the practical follow-through.

Because "you're at risk" is useful information only if you know what to do about it.

The Mahjong Mirror framework was built for exactly this situation — a high-stakes decision where the stakes feel clear but the terrain ahead doesn't. A career crossroads qualifies. Whether you're facing a restructuring, a role that stopped fitting, an entrepreneurial impulse you've been suppressing, or a promotion decision you can't get clear on — the four angles of the mirror are designed to show you what you're not seeing.

I'll walk you through how each angle applies to career decisions specifically.

## The First Angle: Your Central Theme

In any career decision, the first question to answer is: what is this actually about?

Not what it looks like on the surface. What's driving it at the level underneath.

I've done readings for people who came in thinking they had a simple career question — do I take this promotion or not? — and the Central Theme turned out to be about identity. Who they were becoming by saying yes to the offer. Who they'd be leaving behind if they said no. The promotion was just the surface; the identity question was the gravitational force shaping everything.

Other times the Central Theme for a career crossroads is about belonging — the person is thriving technically but has been in the wrong environment for years and can't quite name why nothing feels right. Or it's about timing — the move they want to make is correct, but the Central Theme shows they're moving too early, before something else resolves.

The Central Theme question for career decisions is: what is this crossroads really asking you to resolve? Not the tactical version. The deep version. The answer to that question is the foundation everything else builds on.

## The Second Angle: Looking at Yourself

The second angle is the one people think they've done but usually haven't fully.

Looking at yourself in a career decision means four things: who you fundamentally are, who you need to become, what you actually want, and what's honestly happening in your situation right now.

Most people are good at two of these and avoidant about the other two.

Who you fundamentally are — most people have a reasonable read on this, though it's often obscured by years of adapting to what environments required of them. But the deeper question — who you need to become — is where avoidance usually lives. Because becoming something requires admitting you're not there yet. And admitting you're not there yet means acknowledging a gap that requires work, time, or a humbling.

What you actually want is deceptively hard. Not what you're supposed to want, not what makes sense strategically, not what would impress the right people. What you actually want. Most people, when they sit with this question honestly, find something that surprises them. The Mahjong Mirror creates the conditions to access it.

And what's honestly happening right now — this is where the most avoidance lives in career decisions. People know, usually, more than they admit about their actual situation. The trajectory of their organization. The real opinion their manager has of them. The honest assessment of whether the role they've been pursuing actually fits. The mirror reflects all of it, and what it reflects isn't always what people hoped to see.

## The Third Angle: Your Opposition

This is the angle most people skip entirely, and it's the one that hides the breakthrough.

The Opposition angle reveals what's actually blocking you — not what you think is blocking you, what the tiles reveal. And these are not always the same thing.

People often misidentify their opposition in career decisions. They think the obstacle is external — the company's culture, a difficult manager, the state of the market, the timing. And sometimes it is. But more often, when the tiles show the Third Angle, the real opposition is internal. A belief about what they deserve. A fear that masquerades as pragmatism. A story they tell themselves about why the right move isn't available to them.

In my practice, the people who have the clearest reads on their external opposition — they can describe the obstacles eloquently — often have the least clarity on their internal opposition. The story they've been telling about why things are the way they are is sometimes the most powerful thing blocking them.

The Third Angle doesn't let that stand. It shows you both dimensions — what's actually in the environment and what you're carrying internally that's reinforcing or even creating the situation. In a Fire Horse year, when energy is amplified and decisions have longer-range consequences, the Third Angle is not optional. Skipping it is how people walk into situations they could have seen coming.

## The Fourth Angle: Your Future

The fourth angle is the one that's most misunderstood. People think it's about prediction. It isn't. It's about probability.

The Mahjong Mirror doesn't tell you what will happen. No honest practitioner claims that. What it shows you is the most probable trajectory from where you're currently standing — given your Central Theme, given who you are and who you need to become, given what's blocking you.

Applied to career decisions, the Fourth Angle shows you: if you make this move, where does it most likely take you? Not in a linear, guaranteed sense. In a directional, probabilistic sense. And equally important: if you don't make this move — if you stay, if you wait, if you continue as you are — where does that path most likely lead?

The second question is the one most people forget to ask. They're so focused on whether to take the new opportunity that they don't honestly assess the cost of not taking it. In a Fire Horse year, with disruption running in the background of every career situation, "stay and see what happens" is not a neutral choice. It's a decision with its own probable outcome, and the Fourth Angle shows you that outcome alongside the alternative.

## You Don't Need a Reading to Use This

I want to be direct about something: you don't need to book a reading to use this framework. The book — *The Mahjong Mirror* — walks you through all four angles with enough depth that you can apply them yourself to any significant decision.

That's the whole point. I've spent 35 years helping people see clearly at crossroads. The framework works. It works in readings when I'm reading the tiles. And it works when people take the framework into their own lives and apply the four questions honestly, even without the cards.

What the cards add is depth and a kind of honesty that's harder to access on your own — because when the tiles speak, there's nothing to argue with. You can't talk yourself out of what's in front of you. But the framework itself is completely accessible.

The career crossroads you're navigating right now — whether it's urgent or still on the horizon — is exactly what this framework was built for.
